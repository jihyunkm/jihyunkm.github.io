Scope

While ShopSmiles has high user traffic from social ads, these visits rarely translated to actual purchases.

Meaning, many users visited the site but decided to leave without exploring any other parts of the site or committing to their purchase.

Thus, my Global IT Team was tasked to initiate the first steps of a complete overhaul of the current ShopSmiles website.

With the context in mind, we landed on a core question:

HMW improve ShopSmile's browsing experience to encourage more conversion?

Research

According to past internal audits & heuristic evaluations, we discovered that users were pushed away by product cards.

Product cards touch upon various areas of the site and act as the main drivers of purchase decisions by providing quick gists of information. They allow users to easily compare different products, which is a big user "want."

However, ShopSmiles' product cards were too large and visually distracting‚Äîundermining this value.

I also noted that there were too many variations of the cards throughout the site, making it:

  1. Inconsistent for users
  2. Inconvenient to develop

Before redesigning, I first had to understand the components of a product card.

üí¨

A product card is a container (common region) of related information regarding a product, presenting information in a visually intuitive and digestible way.

A card's content depends on what information users deem most important.

From landscape analyses and user interviews, we concluded that shoppers are most curious of a product's image, name, ratings, and price. The business team also required that we include secondary information such as the sale percentage and the original price, new or best sellers, etc.

I then reduced the number of card variations for a consistent user experience & less development effort.

While 6 variations of the product card lived in the ShopSmiles design system, many were redundant due to similar layouts with minor dimensional differences.

Recognizing this, the cards could quickly be consolidated to 4‚Äîa vertical and horizontal layout for both desktop and mobile screens.

Instead of a slightly longer horizontal card for the desktop Top Picks (PDP), I noticed that we could either use the vertical desktop or the newly consolidated horizontal card, ultimately resulting in 3 card components for ShopSmiles:

  1. Large, vertical (Desktop)
  2. Medium, vertical (Mobile)
  3. Small, horizontal (Desktop & Mobile)

Ideation

The business team's request: as some products are offered in multiple colors, they wanted a color picker featured on the cards.

Previous ShopSmiles' product cards did not show color options, as most of its products are offered in a single color. However, the business team requested a color picker for the products offered in multiple colors, meaning those offered in only one color would instead have to show:

1. A single color selection in the color picker

2. An empty gap with a fixed card height

3. Have no gap with a variable height

Additionally, we decided to display the mobile Top Picks in a carousel instead of a list, changing the card to a vertical layout, to reduce the time it took users to reach the reviews.

This meant that I had to make sure to minimize the number of complex haptic controls (i.e., nested scrolling, pop-up in a scroll), as the color picker would introduce more interactions.

Once the card elements were finalized, I started iterating on the medium-sized product card as it was both the intermediary size and dealt with the most restrictions (as it's suited for mobile screens).

I would then adapt its design to the large and small-sized product card.

Iteration 1: Wide instead of narrow cards

Narrow cards allow for higher comparability (can view more products on a single screen), but hinders the tappability of elements, especially for those with small mobile screens.

Wide cards, on the other hand, might take up more space on the screen, but in turn offer greater accessibility and legibility.

üí°

What I learned...

Here, we have the typical business vs. UX dilemma‚Äîshowing more cards for more opportunities for sale versus the contrast to increase accessibility. However, UX teams should always advocate for the user, even more so when accessibility is involved.

In fact, accessibility -> more users have access -> higher potential for sales! ü§î

Iteration 2: An intuitive color picker

Due to the principles of proximity, the empty gap in place of an inactive color picker looked confusing when placed in a grid.

To avoid this possible confusion, I moved forward with the third option despite the variable height:

  1. A single color selection
  2. An empty gap with a fixed card height
  3. Have no gap with a variable height

To extra-ensure the issue was addressed, we reduced the width of the call-to-action (CTA) on desktop cards, as desktop screens primarily utilized the grid layout. I left the mobile CTA untouched, since (1) no mobile screen used the grid layout and (2) the longer width allowed for a bigger touch area.

Iteration 3: An accessible color picker

However, as we still weren't satisfied with the problem of variable heights, I also came up with a fourth idea: nesting the color selections in a separate button that would prompt to an overlay.

This button would be always docked, solving the problems of an empty gap or misalignment.

While the idea gained support from the engineering team, we dismissed it as it was ultimately inaccessible, especially for those with screen readers.

The design would also: (1) be too complex if embedded within a mobile carousel and (2) feel intrusive, causing frequent UX frustrations.

üí°

What I learned...

While my last idea ultimately turned out to be inaccessible, I still learned that the more ideas, the better:

By ruling out more and more ideas, not only is there more opportunity to justify the best-design-standing, but I can experiment and understand more about the restrictions (here, I learned more about the engineering restrictions).

Implementation

My team eventually fleshed out the entire design system with the new cards.

As previously mentioned, we were able to successfully reduce the component variation of product cards (base state without the color picker) from 6 to 3.

My mentor and I also documented and redlined the new cards, explicitly indicating its usage, spacing, touch areas, and more, for ShopSmiles' developers and any other involved.

Reflection & Takeaways

During my time at Colgate-Palmolive, I not only grew as a designer but gained valuable work ethics and soft skills.

Hands down, my teammates at Colgate's UX Team were one of the most supportive and enjoyable people to work with! Although my time at Colgate was short-lived, I learned so much not only about the design process but the cross-functional nature of UX design.

Some takeaways from my time at Colgate-Palmolive:

Good designers design for everyone.

As a company with a large user base, the UX team was especially concerned with the accessibility of its products. A lot of effort was placed into making our designs accessible, whether it be designing for a screen reader or the smallest mobile screen size possible.

Organization & documentation is key.

Colgate-Palmolive is hundreds of years old, with numerous departments and roles. From this environment, I learned that it's ever-important to keep a record of everything you did and, most importantly, why you did them.

Good organization and documentation not only makes it easier to refer back to one's own work, but makes it easier for others as well‚Äîwhether it be for hand-offs, working in cross-functional environments, or, simply, efficient communication.
