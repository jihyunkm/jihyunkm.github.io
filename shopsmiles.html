Research

üìå According to past internal audits and heuristic evaluations, we discovered that users were pushed away by product cards.

Product cards touch upon various areas of the site and act as the main drivers of purchase decisions by providing quick gists of information. They allow users to easily compare different products, which is a big user "want."

However, ShopSmiles' product cards were too large and visually distracting‚Äîundermining this value.

I also noted that there were too many variations of the cards throughout the site, making it:

  1. Inconsistent for users
  2. Inconvenient to develop

üîç To start redesigning, I first needed to understand the components of a product card.

üí¨

A product card is a container (common region) of related information regarding a product, presenting information in a visually intuitive and digestible way.

A card's content depends on what information users deem most important.

From landscape analyses and user interviews, we concluded that shoppers are most curious of a product's image, name, ratings, and price. The business team also required that we include secondary information such as the sale percentage and the original price, new or best sellers, etc.

My benchmark of e-commerce brands' product cards in use

üî¢ I then sought the minimum number of card variations needed throughout the site.

Currently, 6 variations of the product card live in the ShopSmiles design system. However, I realized that they could easily be reduced to 4, as I boiled their main instances down to the following:

üñ• Desktop

  1. Vertical cards: Home, Search, Product Listing Page
  2. Horizontal cards: Product Description Page (Top Picks), Shopping Cart

üì± Mobile

  1. Vertical cards: Home, Product Listing Page
  2. Horizontal cards: Search, Product Description Page (Top Picks), Shopping Cart

From there, I deduced that we could further lessen the number of cards from 4 to 3. Instead of a slightly longer horizontal card for the desktop Top Picks (PDP), we could use either the vertical desktop or the newly consolidated horizontal card, resulting in 3 product card components for ShopSmiles.

Ideation

üé® The business team's request: as some products are offered in multiple colors, they wanted a color picker featured on the cards.

While we thought we could However, most ShopSmiles products are only offered in a single color and these would instead either show:

1. A single color selection

2. An empty gap with a fixed card height

3. Have no gap with a variable height

Additionally, we decided to display the mobile Top Picks in a carousel instead of a list, changing the card to a vertical layout, to reduce the time it took users to reach the reviews.

This meant that I had to make sure to minimize the number of complex haptic controls (i.e., nested scrolling, pop-up in a scroll), as the color picker would introduce more interactions.

üßë‚Äçüé® With the components of our product card settled, I started exploring various iterations.

1. Wide instead of narrow mobile cards

Narrow cards allow for higher comparability (can view more products on a single screen), but hinders the tappability of elements, especially for those with small mobile screens.

Wide cards, on the other hand, might take up more space on the screen, but in turn offer greater accessibility and legibility.

üí°

What I learned...

Here, we have the typical business vs. UX dilemma‚Äîshowing more cards for more opportunities for sale versus the contrast to increase accessibility. However, UX teams should always advocate for the user, even more so when accessibility is involved.

In fact, accessibility -> more users have access -> higher potential for sales! ü§î

2. An intuitive color picker

Due to the principles of proximity, the empty gap in place of an inactive color picker looked confusing when placed in a grid.

To avoid this possible confusion, I moved forward with the third option despite the variable height:

  1. A single color selection
  2. An empty gap with a fixed card height
  3. Have no gap with a variable height

To extra-ensure the issue was addressed, we reduced the width of the call-to-action (CTA) on desktop cards, as desktop screens primarily utilized the grid layout. I left the mobile CTA untouched, since (1) no mobile screen used the grid layout and (2) the longer width allowed for a bigger touch area.

3. An accessible color picker

However, as we still weren't satisfied with the problem of variable heights, I also came up with a fourth idea: nesting the color selections in a separate button that would prompt to an overlay.

This button would be always docked, solving the problems of an empty gap or misalignment.

While the idea gained support from the engineering team, we dismissed it as it was ultimately inaccessible, especially for those with screen readers.

The design would also: (1) be too complex if embedded within a mobile carousel and (2) feel intrusive, causing frequent UX frustrations.

üí°

What I learned...

While my last idea ultimately turned out to be inaccessible, I still learned that the more ideas, the better:

By ruling out more and more ideas, not only is there more opportunity to justify the best-design-standing, but I can experiment and understand more about the restrictions (here, I learned more about the engineering restrictions).
